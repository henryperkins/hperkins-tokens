AI Leaders, first cohort

I finished as a finalist in the first cohort of AI Leaders — the University of Illinois Chicago and WordPress Foundation program, supported by Automattic. The credential is employer-verifiable, and my portfolio is listed on the program’s showcase. The badge isn’t the part that stuck. The method is. The sharpest pressure on my work came from people building nothing like WordPress — battery governance in rural Mongolia, AI in a classroom — and the shape held every time: a decision is only trustworthy when someone outside the system can inspect it. That’s where “real impact is whatever survives an external instrument” stopped being a line and became how I measure my own work — which is why the record above points at merged changelog lines and open review threads instead of asking you to take my word.

Throughline

Before the AI work, there were service counters, WordPress community rooms, and operations shifts where the system either worked or it did not. Those years shaped the way I build now: make the work concrete, keep the failure modes visible, and explain the tradeoffs to the person who needs the outcome.

What ties my projects together is the same throughline in my support, community, and consulting background: I like taking messy information and turning it into clear workflows, calmer operations, and practical tools that non-specialists can actually use.

WordPress is where my depth is: PageLines developer community work, WordPress.com support at Automattic, client delivery through Lakefront Digital, and current contributions around the platform’s AI stack. The broader pattern is portable: anywhere agents act on live systems, teams need bounded operations, review gates, attribution, and rollback.

I combine support habits, WordPress delivery, TypeScript, and documentation work so teams can ship systems that are easier to maintain, explain, and hand off.

AI Agents & Prompt Engineering

I design agentic workflows — prompt pipelines, reasoning chains, autonomous loops — and the tooling that makes them repeatable. Not demos. Systems that hold up when a real person presses the button.

I built Prompt Forge because I needed a place to design, iterate, and organize prompts at scale. It remains a working system I use to improve prompts without waiting for a single launch milestone.

Technical Consulting & Delivery

Full-arc project ownership: discovery, scoping, build, launch, post-launch. WordPress and WooCommerce platforms. Cloudflare Workers deployments. API integrations and webhook configs. React + TypeScript front ends.

I don’t hand off half-finished work — I ship it, document it, and make sure someone else can maintain it after I leave.

Documentation & Developer Enablement

The best system in the world fails the moment the person who built it walks away. I write the docs, build the onboarding paths, and create the internal tooling that turns tribal knowledge into something a new hire can follow.

I’ve been doing this since I ran a developer community of WordPress professionals at PageLines.
