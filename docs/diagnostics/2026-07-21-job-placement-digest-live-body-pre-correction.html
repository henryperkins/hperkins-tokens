I’ve built in WordPress since 2012 — PageLines first, then client work through Lakefront Digital in Chicago — and the standard I hold the work to is simple: a claim has to survive inspection by someone who isn’t me. The proof I’d point to is HPerkins Tokens, the theme rendering this page right now. It’s a child theme of Automattic’s Assembler where the entire design vocabulary — color, type, spacing — lives in theme.json as named tokens, and everything downstream is a consequence. The stock pickers are off. An editor can’t type a hex value, and the hand-authored CSS aliases onto the variables WordPress generates — the stylesheet points at the tokens instead of keeping its own copy. Drift isn’t discouraged on this site. It can’t happen. View source — the tokens are right there.

Why it matters

Drift is never really a cosmetics problem — it’s a payroll problem. Lock the vocabulary at the schema level and the job stops existing. Off-brand becomes a state the site can’t reach.

Any organization big enough to care about brand is paying someone to catch off-brand work before it ships or clean it up after, and every editor either waits on that person or routes around them. “Are we inside the standard” becomes something you read off the sites instead of asking a team lead. A rebrand collapses from one migration project per site into one edit with a visible blast radius. Editors keep full speed. The brand holds. Nobody takes anybody’s word for it.

The role I’m after is VIP Support Engineer at Automattic — the support rung on purpose — with Solutions Engineer at WordPress VIP as the aim. At VIP, drift doesn’t show up as a governance initiative; it shows up as tickets: this page looks wrong, the rebrand broke our header, who changed the styles and can we get Thursday back. Support is where the problem arrives in the customer’s own words, and an engineer who has built a token system reads those tickets for the missing schema under the symptom. I’ve done Automattic support before — WordPress.com Happiness Engineer, 2012, consumer-side, not enterprise, and I won’t dress it up as more. What it left me with is what a queue teaches: how people actually break things, at volume. Support first, to learn what VIP’s customers break. Then the SE seat.

Non-negotiables: three questions, any no kills the posting

The opening statement makes three claims. Each one is a screen. A posting that fails any of them doesn’t get an hour of my time, whatever it pays.

Will the work survive inspection by someone who isn’t me? That’s the standard in the first paragraph, and it only functions where the output is inspectable — merged PRs, tagged releases, live sites, changelogs a stranger can load. A role where impact is self-reported — decks, dashboards, “drove alignment” — can’t use the standard and can’t extend the record. One question, most of the feed gone.

Is the customer’s problem operational WordPress at scale — a payroll problem wearing a technical symptom? The second paragraph is the claim: drift costs organizations a standing job, not ugly pages. The work I’m built for sits where the platform meets the organization paying to keep it inside a standard. A posting about one site, or a stack where WordPress is incidental, fails this even when the title says engineer.

Is this a rung or a detour? The closing paragraph is the path: support first, to learn what VIP’s customers break, then the SE seat. So the test for any posting is whether it moves me along that line — enterprise WordPress customers, met from the inside, in their own vocabulary — or just pays. A posting can clear the first two questions and still fail this one. Salary doesn’t answer it.

The validated set: three questions against the live market

The three questions above ran against roughly forty live postings — Automattic and WordPress VIP, the enterprise agencies, the managed hosts, publishers running WordPress at scale. Sixteen passed the AI pre-screen. Thirteen survived my review; the three overturns stay in the sheet with their reasons, because a screen you can't watch working is a screen you take on faith.

The catch worth naming: the tool passed Automattic's Happiness Engineer role while its own rationale carried the kill — consumer, single-site — and quoted my own distinction between consumer support and enterprise support back at me. The company name had answered a question about the customer. I overturned it.

Then the link check made its own point: between the morning screen and the afternoon verification, four validated postings closed without a word. Rolling deadlines roll shut silently. The full sheet renders every state — live, delisted, dropped — with dates.

Screening notes, because a constraint claim is only as strong as its denominator. Two passes ran on July 18, 2026 — roughly sixty-five postings examined across Automattic and WordPress VIP, the VIP partner agencies, the managed hosts, media organizations running WordPress platform teams, and the general boards. The first pass produced sixteen AI passes; my review dropped three; liveness verification the same afternoon found four more delisted or replaced, leaving eight confirmed live and one behind a bot-wall pending a browser check. The second pass — twenty-five more postings and boards, wider — returned zero clean passes and two borderlines: an evergreen listing gated to Asia, and an onsite-only Los Angeles seat whose canonical posting wouldn't load to inclusion standard.

So the digest ships at the market's number, not the exemplar's. The live, US-remote, WordPress-at-enterprise-scale rung is thin right now — historically reliable sources are dead or evergreen — and I won't stretch the second and third questions to manufacture rows; that's the exact failure mode the catch above documents. The screen isn't finished with the market either: the WordPress product-company boards and the Post Status job board are unscreened, named as next-pass targets, and the delisted Automattic reqs recycle, so the board gets re-read. What's in the sheet is what survived the questions today, dated, with every state visible.
